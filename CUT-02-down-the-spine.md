# CUT 2 — DOWN THE SPINE
### NOW → the first descent · the book's own binding · the veil goes up
*Wound map, cut 2. Deeper than the arm. Opens the first real fall into the boy's dark — nothing named, everything felt. Black, white, and blood. The veil is law here: not one fact touches the page.*

---

**NOW. LATER. THE SAME RAIN, HEAVIER.**

The arm's wrapped. He's still bleeding through it, a slow red dot growing on the grey, but he's up, he's working, because the file is open on the desk now and a man like this doesn't leave a thing like that open and walk away. He reads with the good hand. **Weld** stands at his shoulder — the hand that does the work, the one that fixes what's broke, that's never once met a busted thing it couldn't put back together.

Weld flexes. Weld wants in. *Point me at it,* Weld says. *Whatever did this. I'll make it right. That's what I'm for.*

The man doesn't answer, because the man has already read far enough down the page to know the thing Weld hasn't figured out yet: some of this is already done. You can't weld a door that closed thirty years ago and you can't weld one that closed last month on somebody you'd have died to stand in front of.

**THE SECOND CUT**

It comes from behind again. They always come from behind for this one. A hand fists the back of his coat, hauls the fabric up to bare the spine — the oldest ritual there is, older than he is — and then the line.

**Down the spine.**

Not a stab. A *drawing.* A slow bright box-cutter line pulled down the length of him, vertebra by vertebra, so soft-feeling and so wrong, the warm coming up before the pain files its report. Red running into his belt. The single color in the room, tracing the exact old seam it's tracing because that seam has been there since a highway — the box-cutter line, the binding, the crease the whole book is folded along.

And when steel meets that seam, the book *opens.*

**THE VEIL GOES UP**

He expects the white-out. The horns. '96, the way the arm gave him '96.

He doesn't get '96.

He gets somewhere he's never been and has been drowning in for weeks. He falls straight down through his own oldest scar and comes out the bottom into a dark that isn't his.

*Nothing here has edges. Nothing here has a name.* A room, maybe. A light that calls itself a light and isn't — a bulb where a moon should hang, dead still, lying about being the moon. He feels a good and honest structure standing in that false light with all its doors unlocked because it never learned it needed locks. He feels the windows going black one at a time under a patient brush. He feels a weather that isn't weather come down grey and chemical and smother a true sky. He feels rest get outlawed — *not allowed to close, not allowed to close, wake up* — and he feels a cathedral learn, slow, nightly, that the red on its own floor is nothing to cry about.

He can't see any of it. That's the horror of the veil. He can only *feel* it, the way you feel a room you've walked into in the pitch dark and know is full of something. Everything felt. Nothing shown. No face on the thing. No word for what it did. Just a father, falling through the deepest wound of his own body, landing inside a dark that was done to somebody he made — and finding, when he reaches for it —

**WELD, IN THE DARK, USELESS FOR THE FIRST TIME**

— nothing to grip. Weld's down there with him now, the good hand, the fixer, reaching into the felt-dark for a seam to close, a bolt to set, a thing to *make right* —

and there's no metal in it. There's no edge to weld. You cannot repair a wound that happened to someone else in a room you weren't allowed into. The hand that has never failed opens and closes on nothing, on smoke, on borrowed grey weather, and something in Weld that has never once broken makes a sound going down.

*This is the why,* the dark says. Not cruel. Just true. *You wanted to know why every cut in your body still bleeds. This is why. You've been bleeding down toward this the whole time.*

**BACK UP**

He claws out the way you claw out of deep water with one working arm — too slow, lungs screaming — and breaks the surface into NOW, into the rain, face down over his own desk, the red from the arm and the red from the spine pooling together now on a nameless grey folder until you can't tell the two wounds apart. Old blood and new. His and the boy's. The same red. It was always going to be the same red.

**BRACE. HARDER.**

Cut one taught him the blades have doors. Cut two teaches him what's behind the doors, and it's worse than his own history — it's the history he couldn't stop.

At the edge of the frame the hand still hasn't moved. Still ringless. Still patient. Still waiting exactly where something was already going to swim.

*That was the second one,* the dark says. *You've got four scars left. You know exactly how deep they go.*

**HOLD ON THE TWO REDS RUNNING TOGETHER. FADE.**

---

### Note on placement
Cut 2 of the wound map — the pivot from *his* pain to the *boy's.* The engine bends here on purpose: cut 1 dropped to '96; cut 2 was expected to drop to '96 (it's the spine, the origin wound) and instead drops sideways into the boy's dark — because the spine is the binding, and cutting the binding opens the whole book. The veil is total: the Architect/phantom is present entirely as feeling — false light, blacked windows, borrowed weather, outlawed sleep — and not one fact, name, or act is shown. Weld's first failure carries the tragedy of crew-as-mind: the part of him built to fix things cannot fix what was done to his son. The two bloods running together (his '96, the boy's now) sets the book's thesis: same wound, same red, one spine.
